# Research Synthesis: Empire-Landpower

## 1. WHAT THE RESEARCH FOUND

**The research found substantial engagement with Giovanni Arrighi's framework of systemic cycles of accumulation, but limited direct evidence addressing the core "positional swap" question as framed.** Across 13 sessions, the most consistently surfaced sources were Arrighi's *The Long Twentieth Century* and related work on hegemonic transitions. These sources provide a theoretical framework for understanding how dominant powers transfer strategic capacity to successors—but the research did not surface direct empirical studies of the US-China manufacturing transfer as a discrete historical event comparable to prior hegemonic transitions.

**The Mackinder thread produced applications rather than serious theoretical challenges.** Multiple sessions found sources applying Mackinder's heartland theory to contemporary geopolitics (Chowdhury & Hel Kafi 2015 on Central Asia; Cannon 2025 on Russia-China partnership), but the research explicitly noted these are "straightforward applications rather than serious challenges or updates to the theory itself." The most interesting finding was Oliver Krause's (2023) German-language work examining how Mackinder was *instrumentalized* by US policy establishments—a meta-question about how theories become tools rather than whether the theory is analytically correct. Russian scholarship (Gadzhiev's *Introduction to Geopolitics*) critiques Mackinder as geographical determinism, but this was flagged for follow-up rather than substantively analyzed.

**Latin American dependency theory emerged as the strongest non-Anglophone scholarly tradition engaged—and its implications for the positional swap thesis are more direct than the synthesis initially credited.** The research surfaced Portuguese and Spanish-language sources (Martins via CLACSO; Treacy 2022 on Marini's sub-imperialism) that do not merely complicate the framing of hegemonic transfers as contingent strategic choices—they structurally undermine it. The Martins/Marini argument is that core-periphery positions are reproduced regardless of which state occupies the core: the center changes, the periphery remains, and the mechanisms of extraction travel with the position rather than the nation. This is not an indirect challenge to the positional swap thesis; it is a direct one, asserting that the swap itself may be epiphenomenal to the deeper structure. Barbara Stallings' work testing whether China reproduces US dependency mechanisms in Latin America was identified as an empirical test case for exactly this claim, though the research notes this is US-based analysis rather than Latin American scholarship proper.

**Critical gaps were explicitly identified and not filled.** Session 001 directly states: "No direct non-Anglophone takes on the 'positional swap' as Kotkin frames it were found." The research flagged Chinese-language sources as a gap requiring Session 002 follow-up. The NBR project (2024) analyzing Chinese strategists' assessments of Russia was noted as adjacent but insufficient—it is US analysis *of* Chinese scholarship, not Chinese scholarship itself. The loyalty-competence literature (Zakharov 2016; 2022 ScienceDirect paper on CCP cadre selection) was found to be "solid but English-dominated," with the Chinese institutional context identified as the missing piece.

## 2. WHERE THE HYPOTHESIS HOLDS

The research provides **moderate support for the existence of historical precedents for industrial/strategic capacity transfer** through Arrighi's framework. The systemic cycles of accumulation model explicitly addresses how hegemonic powers financialize while transferring productive capacity to successors—this pattern (Genoese → Dutch → British → American) is well-documented in the sources found.

**The dependency theory finding offers direct, not merely indirect, support for a structural reading of the positional swap.** If Martins and the CLACSO tradition are correct that core-periphery relations are reproduced regardless of which nation occupies the core, then the positional swap may be structurally determined rather than a policy failure. This simultaneously *strengthens* one version of Kotkin's concern—the outcome was structurally overdetermined—while *undermining* another version—that it was a correctable mistake attributable to US strategic error. The dependency framework reframes the question: not whether the US handed China capacity it didn't have to, but whether any hegemon in the financialization phase can prevent productive capacity from migrating to its successor.

**Mackinder's framework remains analytically live** in the sense that contemporary scholars (Cannon 2025) argue the Russia-China partnership embodies exactly what Mackinder feared. The research did not find serious refutations of the heartland thesis, only applications and meta-critiques about its political uses.

# Devil's Advocate Challenge: Empire-Landpower Synthesis

## 1. WHERE IT DOESN'T HOLD

**The synthesis claims moderate support for the positional swap thesis, but the evidence actually points toward theoretical frameworks that *explain* hegemonic transitions, not evidence that the US-China case *fits* those frameworks.**

The most significant problem: Arrighi's systemic cycles model describes transitions between maritime-commercial hegemons (Genoa → Netherlands → Britain → US). China is not a maritime-commercial hegemon ascending through financialization of its predecessor. It is, in Mackinder's terms, a *landpower*—and the research surfaced zero sources addressing whether Arrighi's framework applies to a landpower successor at all. The synthesis treats this as a gap to be filled; I would argue it's a potential category error at the heart of the research.

**The dependency theory finding actually *undermines* the research framing more than the synthesis acknowledges.** If Martins and the CLACSO tradition are correct that core-periphery positions are structurally reproduced regardless of which nation occupies the core, then the entire "positional swap" question becomes analytically incoherent. You cannot "hand over" something that was never yours to give—the productive capacity migrates according to structural logic, not policy choice. The synthesis frames this as "simultaneously strengthening and undermining" Kotkin's concern, but that's diplomatic hedging. If the structural argument holds, there is no swap to analyze, only a transition to observe. The research should have confronted this more directly.

**The Mackinder thread is weaker than presented.** The synthesis states "the research did not find serious refutations of the heartland thesis, only applications and meta-critiques." But absence of refutation in a 13-session research project is not evidence of validity—it may simply mean the research didn't look in the right places. Gadzhiev's critique was "flagged for follow-up rather than substantively analyzed." That's not a finding; that's a deferral. The synthesis cannot claim Mackinder "remains analytically live" while simultaneously acknowledging the main critique was never examined.

## 2. ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATIONS

**The Arrighi framework may be descriptive rather than predictive.** The synthesis treats the systemic cycles model as providing "historical precedents" for the US-China transfer. But Arrighi was writing retrospective history, not forecasting. The fact that prior transitions followed a pattern does not mean the current transition will—especially given the landpower/seapower distinction the research never addressed. The same evidence could support a reading that Arrighi's framework is inapplicable to the current case precisely because China breaks the pattern.

**The dependency theory sources may be ideologically motivated rather than empirically grounded.** The CLACSO tradition emerged from a specific political project (Latin American anti-imperialism). Martins' argument that "the center changes, the periphery remains" is a structural claim, but it's also a political one that serves particular interests. The synthesis accepts this framing uncritically. An alternative reading: the dependency school's insistence that hegemonic identity doesn't matter is itself a form of motivated reasoning that obscures real differences in how different hegemons treat peripheries.

**The "instrumentalization" finding (Krause 2023) could be read as evidence that Mackinder is analytically *exhausted* rather than analytically live.** If the primary scholarly activity around Mackinder is studying how policymakers *use* the theory rather than whether the theory is *correct*, that suggests the theory has migrated from analytical tool to rhetorical device. The synthesis frames this as an interesting meta-question; it could equally be read as evidence that Mackinder has become unfalsifiable and therefore analytically useless.

## 3. WHAT YOU DIDN'T EXPECT

**The research surfaced a potential contradiction it didn't resolve: the framing assumes the "positional swap" is a discrete event, but the strongest sources found (Arrighi, Martins) describe structural processes that unfold over decades or centuries.** If the swap is structural, it's not an event that can be dated, blamed, or reversed. This creates a temporal mismatch between the research question (implicitly: when did this happen and who's responsible?) and the theoretical frameworks that emerged (implicitly: this is how capitalism works).

**The Latin American dependency thread is more interesting than the synthesis credits—but for a different reason.** The Stallings work testing whether China reproduces US dependency mechanisms is not just an empirical test of structural reproduction. It's a test of whether *any* core power can behave differently. If China does reproduce the mechanisms, that supports structural determinism. If China behaves differently, that challenges it. The synthesis notes this but doesn't highlight that this is potentially the most important empirical question the research identified.

**The loyalty-competence literature (Zakharov) appears entirely disconnected from the core question.** The synthesis notes it was "found but not connected." This suggests either scope creep in the research or an implicit hypothesis that authoritarian governance dynamics affect strategic capacity transfer. If the latter, the connection was never made explicit. If the former, it's noise.

## 4. REVISED FRAMING

The original motivation was listed as "To be written"—which means the research may have proceeded without a clear hypothesis to test. This is a significant methodological problem that the synthesis noted but did not adequately address.

**If the implicit question was "Did the US hand China strategic capacity it didn't have to?":**
The research found no direct evidence addressing this question. It found theoretical frameworks (Arrighi, dependency theory) that either make the question unanswerable (structural determinism) or reframe it entirely (hegemonic transition as systemic pattern). The revised framing should be: *Is the US-China industrial transfer analytically comparable to prior hegemonic transitions, or does China's landpower character make it a different kind of case?* This question was never asked and never answered.

**If the implicit question was "How do non-Anglophone scholars understand this dynamic?":**
The research found Latin American sources that challenge the framing and flagged Chinese and Russian sources it never examined. The revised framing should be: *What do Chinese scholars themselves argue about China's acquisition of industrial capacity from the US?* This is the gap the synthesis identifies but understates—it's not a gap, it's the missing center of the research.

**Prompt to researcher:** The strongest finding here may be negative: the theoretical frameworks you found (Arrighi, dependency theory) either don't apply to the landpower case or render the "positional swap" question incoherent. Before proceeding, you need to decide: Are you investigating a policy failure (which requires evidence it was avoidable) or a structural transition (which requires different analytical tools entirely)? The research cannot answer both questions simultaneously, and it's currently trying to.
