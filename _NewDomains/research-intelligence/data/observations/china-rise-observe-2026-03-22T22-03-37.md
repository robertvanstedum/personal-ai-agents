# Synthesis: China-Rise

This set frames China's rise not as an isolated phenomenon but as a structural moment within long-cycle theories of hegemonic transition and dependency. The core tension is whether China represents a **successor hegemon** following predictable patterns (Arrighi) or whether it's navigating a fundamentally different structural position shaped by past dependency and the Soviet precedent.

## Strongest argument

**Arrighi's systemic cycles framework** provides the most analytically powerful lens here. His model of hegemonic transitions — where financial expansion signals terminal decline of the incumbent and productive relocation to a rising power — maps cleanly onto US deindustrialization and China's manufacturing dominance. The pattern (Dutch → British → American) suggests China isn't an anomaly but the next iteration. This is your structural anchor.

## Key tension

**Martins vs. Arrighi on China's structural position.** Martins argues dependency relations are **designed features** of the system, not bugs — meaning China's integration into global capitalism may constrain rather than enable hegemonic succession. Arrighi's framework assumes the rising power can leverage productive capacity into systemic leadership, but Martins suggests the very structures that enabled China's growth (export-led development, dollar dependence, technology gaps) may prevent full hegemonic transition. This is unresolved in your set.

## The Kotkin wildcard

Kotkin introduces a **non-economic constraint**: regime survival. His focus on Xi avoiding Soviet collapse suggests China's leadership prioritizes **political durability over hegemonic ambition**. This cuts against both Arrighi (who assumes rising powers pursue systemic leadership) and Martins (who focuses on economic structure). If the CCP's primary goal is not-collapsing rather than displacing the US, the entire hegemonic transition frame may misread Chinese strategy.

## The Mackinder piece

**Krause is the outlier** — it's about how geopolitical theories function as **legitimation tools** for policy, not about China directly. But it's potentially the most methodologically important: it warns against treating Arrighi's cycles or dependency theory as objective descriptions rather than interpretive frameworks that can themselves become policy instruments.

Does a Leninist party-state follow the same financial expansion → decline pattern, or does political control over capital flows break the cycle? You need sources on **Chinese state capitalism** and whether it represents a **different hegemonic form** or a **non-hegemonic alternative**.
