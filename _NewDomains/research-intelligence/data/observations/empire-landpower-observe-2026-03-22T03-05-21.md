# Synthesis: Empire-Landpower

These three sources converge on a single structural claim: **geopolitical theories don't just describe power — they constitute it**. The set moves from Arrighi's macro-historical cycles, through Krause's analysis of how geographic determinism becomes policy doctrine, to Martins' argument that asymmetry is systemic architecture, not accident.

## Core convergence

Arrighi provides the temporal scaffolding: hegemonic transitions follow capital accumulation cycles, with declining powers financializing while rising challengers build productive capacity. Krause shows how Mackinder's heartland theory gave US policymakers a ready-made narrative framework that turned geographic anxiety into interventionist legitimation — the theory didn't need to be *true* to be *useful*. Martins completes the picture: what looks like market failure (persistent dependency, uneven development) is actually the global system functioning correctly to reproduce hierarchy.

Together, they suggest **US-China competition isn't a deviation from rules-based order but the latest iteration of how hegemonic systems work**: the incumbent financializes and ideologizes (Arrighi), deploys geographic determinism to justify containment (Krause), while structural dependency mechanisms ensure challengers can't simply copy the path to dominance (Martins).

## Tension

The set has an unresolved gap on **agency versus structure**. Arrighi's cycles imply near-mechanical transitions driven by capital logic. Krause emphasizes how elites *choose* to weaponize certain theories over others — Mackinder won, Haushofer lost, for contingent political reasons. Martins sits between them: dependency is structural, but he doesn't address whether counter-hegemonic strategy can exploit cycle timing (Arrighi) or narrative contestation (Krause).

## What's missing

**Landpower itself barely appears as a material variable**. Krause treats Mackinder as discourse, not assessment of whether rail networks and resource access actually matter. Arrighi's cycles are capital-driven; territorial control is derivative. Martins focuses on economic extraction, not geographic control. If you're researching "empire-landpower," this set tells you how theories *about* landpower function ideologically, but not whether contemporary landpower — Chinese Belt and Road infrastructure, Russian resource leverage, US base networks — operates differently than 20th-century precedents.

## Most promising thread

**How does the current hegemon's narrative toolkit differ from previous transitions?** Krause shows Mackinder worked for Cold War containment. What's the equivalent geographic/strategic theory legitimating US China policy now? Is it "Indo-Pacific" framing, supply chain resilience, tech chokepoints? And does China's lack of a comparable *universalizing* geographic theory (versus tributary-zone pragmatism) represent strategic vulnerability or evidence that this transition works differently?
