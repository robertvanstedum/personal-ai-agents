# Synthesis: Empire-Landpower

These three sources converge on a single analytical move: **reframing hegemonic transitions and geopolitical doctrine as structural features of capitalism, not contingent policy choices or timeless strategic truths.**

## Core argument

Arrighi provides the temporal architecture: hegemonic cycles are systemic, not accidental. Each hegemon (Genoa, Dutch, British, American) follows a predictable sequence — material expansion, financialization, crisis, displacement. The US-China transition isn't unprecedented; it's the fourth iteration of a 700-year pattern. The implication: current US policy debates treat as novel what is structurally inevitable.

Krause shows how geopolitical *theory* itself becomes a legitimation instrument. Mackinder's Heartland thesis wasn't adopted because it was empirically superior — it was weaponized because it justified specific US interventions. The theory's truth-value mattered less than its political utility. This cuts against taking any geopolitical framework (including Mackinder, Mahan, Spykman) at face value.

Martins extends this to North-South relations: dependency isn't a development failure but the global economy functioning correctly. Core economies *require* peripheral extraction. What looks like policy failure is structural necessity.

## Tension

There's an unresolved methodological friction. Arrighi's longue durée cycles assume **regularities across centuries** — hegemonic transitions follow laws. But Krause demonstrates that the **geopolitical concepts used to explain those transitions** are historically contingent political tools, not natural laws. If Mackinder was adopted for legitimation rather than explanatory power, what prevents Arrighi's cycle theory from being the same? The sources don't address whether structural Marxist frameworks escape their own critique of geopolitical realism.

## Gap

None of these sources adequately theorize **landpower specifically**. Arrighi focuses on maritime/commercial hegemons. Krause discusses Mackinder's Heartland but only as discourse, not material reality. Martins addresses extraction but not territorial control. The research topic implies interest in continental empires (Russia? China's Belt and Road as landpower projection?), but this set doesn't yet engage what makes *land-based* hegemony structurally different from sea-based.
