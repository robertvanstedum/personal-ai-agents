## Core argument across sources

**Hegemonic decline follows a financial pattern.** Arrighi's systemic cycles framework shows dominant powers shift from productive to financial accumulation as they lose competitive edge — a "signal crisis" where the hegemon financializes rather than produces. The 1971 Nixon shock (source 5) fits this template precisely: the US abandoned gold convertibility when it could no longer sustain the Bretton Woods system, marking America's shift from industrial dominance to dollar-denominated financial control. This wasn't policy failure; Martins would argue it's the system working as designed — maintaining structural advantage through different means.

**Theory becomes weapon.** Krause's analysis of Mackinder adds a critical layer: geopolitical frameworks don't just describe power transitions, they *legitimate* them. The heartland theory justified US Cold War policy regardless of analytical validity. This matters for gold-geopolitics because it suggests current narratives about dollar dominance, dedollarization, or gold's return aren't neutral observations — they're positioning moves in a transition struggle.

**The China question looms.** Kotkin's Soviet-China comparison (source 4) asks whether China can navigate hegemonic transition differently than the USSR. The USSR collapsed; China studies that collapse obsessively. If Arrighi is right about cyclical patterns, China faces the question: can a rising power break the cycle, or will it reproduce the same financial-then-decline trajectory?

## The gap

None of these sources examine what happens to gold *during* the transition phase — whether rising powers accumulate it, whether it becomes a hedge against dollar dominance, or whether it's simply irrelevant in a world of fiat currency competition.
