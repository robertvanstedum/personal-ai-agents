# Synthesis: Empire-Landpower

These three sources converge on a shared analytical move: **reframing geopolitical competition not as contingent policy choices but as structural features of capitalist hegemonic cycles**. Each operates at a different level of abstraction, but together they form a coherent critique of liberal internationalist narratives that treat great power rivalry as aberration rather than system logic.

## Core argument

**Arrighi** provides the temporal scaffolding: hegemonic transitions follow predictable phases—material expansion, financial expansion, systemic chaos—driven by accumulation crises, not ideological preference. The US-China transition maps cleanly onto this cycle, with US financialization signaling late-stage hegemony and Chinese manufacturing dominance echoing British-to-American handover dynamics.

**Martins** supplies the structural mechanism: dependency isn't market failure but **designed hierarchy**. Core-periphery relations aren't bugs in globalization; they're load-bearing features that enable accumulation. This directly challenges reformist framings (trade deals, development aid) by arguing the system requires stratification to function.

**Krause** reveals the ideological layer: geopolitical theories like Mackinder's Heartland don't gain traction because they're analytically sound—they gain traction because they **legitimate specific policy agendas**. The theory becomes a political instrument, naturalizing contestation over Eurasian landmass control as strategic necessity rather than hegemonic preservation.

## Tension

The set lacks empirical grounding in **landpower specifics**. Arrighi and Martins operate at high systemic altitude; Krause critiques Mackinder's *use* without testing whether land-based resource control actually matters differently than sea-based trade dominance in the current cycle. If Chinese Belt and Road represents landpower strategy (overland Eurasian integration), does this constitute a structural break from prior maritime hegemonies, or just tactical variation within the same accumulation logic?

## Strongest thread to pull

**The missing piece is mechanism**: *How does landpower shift hegemonic transition dynamics compared to seapower-based cycles?* 

Arrighi's cycles are derived from maritime empires (Genoa, Netherlands, Britain, US). If China's rise depends on **territorial integration** (infrastructure, resource corridors, physical connectivity) rather than oceanic trade monopoly, does this alter crisis timing, vulnerability points, or transition violence? The research gap is empirical: what does landpower *do* in a hegemonic transition that seapower doesn't, and does Arrighi's cycle phasing hold when the challenger's accumulation base is continental rather than naval?
