**The "willing to suffer" assumption may be backwards.** Your motivation assumes regime willingness translates to population tolerance. But Iran's 2019 fuel price protests (which the synthesis notes were not researched) saw nationwide unrest suppressed only through internet blackouts and lethal force killing hundreds. The regime's *willingness* to impose suffering is not the same as its *capacity* to survive it. Authoritarian regimes fall precisely when economic crisis meets security force defection—a dynamic your framing doesn't address.

The U.S. Fifth Fleet operates from Bahrain specifically for this contingency. Historical precedent (Operation Praying Mantis, 1988) saw Iranian naval assets destroyed within hours. The regime cannot simultaneously weaponize the strait and fund itself. This creates a self-defeating logic your motivation doesn't account for.

## 2. ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATIONS — Same Evidence, Different Reading

The evidence that "the Strait remains critical amid regional conflict" could be read **against** your hypothesis:

- **Criticality creates deterrence, not leverage.** The very fact that Hormuz remains open during intense regional conflict (Yemen, Gaza, Iran-Israel tensions) suggests all parties—*including Iran*—recognize that closure triggers escalation they cannot control. Iran's restraint despite motivation may indicate they've already concluded they *cannot* hold for months.

- **Global adaptation capacity is underestimated.** The chokepoint literature emphasizes vulnerability but not resilience. The 2022 Russia-Ukraine disruption showed markets adapt faster than expected through demand destruction, SPR releases, and supply substitution. "Unacceptable costs" may be shorter-lived than assumed.

- **The game may not be closure at all.** Iranian strategic communications consistently threaten Hormuz but haven't executed. The *threat* may be the weapon—maintaining leverage without incurring costs. Your framing assumes they'll actually close it; their revealed preferences suggest otherwise.
