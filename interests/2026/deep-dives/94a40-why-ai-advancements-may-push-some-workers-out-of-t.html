1. Why This Matters

The St. Louis Fed is signaling that AI displacement may force workers to permanently exit employment rather than transition to new roles‚Äîa fundamental shift from historical automation patterns. This validates concerns about structural unemployment rather than just temporary displacement, with significant implications for social safety nets, labor force participation rates, and inequality.

2. Core Argument

The Fed's model suggests AI improvements create a "skill obsolescence" effect where certain labor types become economically unviable, not just temporarily displaced. Unlike previous automation waves, AI's ability to perform cognitive tasks may eliminate entire occupational categories without creating equivalent substitute roles at the same skill levels.

3. Contrarian Take

  • Historical precedent: Every major technological shift (electricity, computers, internet) initially appeared job-destroying but ultimately expanded employment through productivity gains and new sectors
  • Political Economy blind spot: The model assumes efficient market clearing; it ignores that employment outcomes depend heavily on policy interventions, retraining programs, and redistribution mechanisms that can prevent labor force exit
